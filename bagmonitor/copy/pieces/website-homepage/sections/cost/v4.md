# The Cost of No Visibility — v4

**Eyebrow:** The Cost

**Heading:** The Cost of Lost Visibility

**Intro:**
The airline industry spends approximately $5 billion annually on mishandled baggage — 33.4 million events in 2024 alone. A single mishandling event generates three separate, compounding costs. Most internal estimates capture only the first.

---

**Operational cost**
The confirmed floor for a mishandling event is ~$150: courier transport, staff hours, and claims processing overhead. Field-validated by Sky Airlines (Hub Operations, Santiago/Lima) and Aegean Airlines (Head of Ground Ops, Athens), this is the cost that activates before the bag's location is known and before any compensation claim is filed.

**Tracing cost**
Every bag whose location becomes unknown triggers a $22 WorldTracer activation fee — the cost of initiating SITA's trace-and-match workflow. This is conditional: it applies only when the airline cannot already account for the bag. But WorldTracer operates downstream of the failure, not upstream of it; by the time it activates, the manual labor, call center volume, and courier dispatches that compound the per-event cost are already running.

**Compensation exposure**
The Montreal Convention caps international airline liability at ~$2,091 per passenger (1,519 SDRs, effective December 2024). On US domestic routes, 14 CFR §254.4 sets a $4,700 minimum — a floor, not a ceiling, with no limit on proven damages. That asymmetry means domestic exposure is 2.3× the international cap on routes where losses are easier to prevent. Seventy-four percent of mishandled bags are delays rather than permanent losses, but each still generates a full claims workflow.

**Passenger trust**
Forty-seven percent of passengers say real-time mobile tracking would increase their confidence in checking a bag; 38% say digital identification raises their trust in the airline (SITA 2025). Sky Airlines and Iberia both named NPS as a distinct economic consequence of mishandling — one that does not appear in the claims ledger but affects future check-in decisions, ancillary revenue, and whether a delay event escalates into a loss claim. An airline without baggage visibility cannot communicate proactively during a failure; a passenger who discovers the problem at baggage claim has already formed their opinion.
