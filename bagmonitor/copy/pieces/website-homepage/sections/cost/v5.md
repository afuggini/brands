# The Cost of No Visibility — v5

**Eyebrow:** The Cost

**Heading:** The Cost of Lost Visibility

**Intro:**
The airline industry spends approximately $5 billion annually on mishandled baggage — 33.4 million events in 2024 alone. A single mishandling event generates three separate, compounding costs. Understanding the stack is the first step to quantifying what prevention is actually worth.

---

**Operational cost**
When a bag is mishandled, an airline dispatches staff to trace it, arranges courier transport to return it, and opens a claims file. That process has a known cost: approximately $150 per event, according to IATA's industry reference — a figure confirmed independently in field conversations with Sky Airlines and Aegean Airlines. This is the operational floor. It applies before the bag's location is known, before tracing is initiated, and before a single compensation claim is considered.

**Recovery overhead**
WorldTracer is the industry's standard mechanism for tracing bags whose location is unknown. Each activation costs $22 — a conditional fee that applies only when the airline has lost track of a bag entirely. That $22 is the visible portion; the underlying labor — agents, calls, courier coordination — extends the real per-event cost well beyond the fee. WorldTracer operates strictly downstream of the failure: it is the cost of searching, not the cost of preventing.

**Compensation exposure**
International airline liability for mishandled baggage is capped by the Montreal Convention at 1,519 SDRs per passenger — approximately $2,091 USD at current rates, effective December 2024. US domestic routes operate under a different framework: 14 CFR §254.4 sets a $4,700 minimum with no ceiling on proven damages, making domestic liability structurally higher than the international cap. The compensation layer applies on top of operational and tracing costs, and it activates on every event where a passenger files a claim — which includes 74% of events classified as delays, not just losses.

**Reputational cost**
Baggage mishandling does not end as a line item. Forty-seven percent of passengers say real-time tracking would make them more confident checking a bag; 38% say a digital identification system increases their trust in the airline (SITA 2025). In field conversations, both Sky Airlines and Iberia cited NPS as a direct economic consequence of baggage failures — one that compounds over time through reduced check-in rates, lower ancillary revenue, and the pattern Iberia named explicitly: delay events that get escalated and filed as loss claims by passengers who don't distinguish between the two.
