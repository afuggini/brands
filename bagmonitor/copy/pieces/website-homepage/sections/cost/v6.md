# The Cost of No Visibility — v6

**Eyebrow:** The Cost

**Heading:** The Cost of Lost Visibility

**Intro:**
The airline industry spends approximately $5 billion annually on mishandled baggage — 33.4 million events in 2024 alone. A single mishandling event generates three separate, compounding costs. None of them disappear when visibility is absent — they are absorbed by whoever has the least data.

---

**Operational cost**
Before the airline knows where the bag is, it has already spent approximately $150: courier logistics, staff time, and claims overhead. This is the confirmed operational floor — IATA's industry reference, validated in the field by Sky Airlines and Aegean Airlines, both of which use the same figure in their internal cost models. It is the baseline every mishandling event starts from, regardless of whether the bag is eventually found.

**Recovery overhead**
When the airline cannot locate a bag, it opens a WorldTracer case at $22 per activation. That fee covers access to SITA's trace-and-match system; the labor behind the recovery — agents, calls, courier dispatches — adds to the total without appearing in that number. The critical point: WorldTracer is the cost of not knowing. It does not exist as a line item for airlines that know where their bags are.

**Compensation exposure**
When a passenger files a claim, the exposure is jurisdiction-dependent but material in every market. Internationally, the Montreal Convention caps liability at ~$2,091 per passenger (1,519 SDRs, effective December 2024). On US domestic routes, federal regulation sets a $4,700 minimum — with no ceiling — making domestic exposure 2.3× the international cap. Seventy-four percent of mishandling events are delays rather than permanent losses, but each one can trigger the same claims workflow; Iberia has noted that passengers routinely escalate delay events to loss claims when the airline cannot proactively account for the bag.

**Reputational cost**
The cost that does not appear on the claims ledger is the one that compounds. Forty-seven percent of passengers say real-time tracking would make them more confident checking a bag; 38% say digital identification increases their trust in the airline (SITA 2025). Sky Airlines and Iberia both raised NPS — unprompted — as a distinct economic consequence of mishandling, separate from the operational and compensation costs. The mechanism is straightforward: passengers who lose confidence in baggage handling check fewer bags, choose different carriers for future trips, and file claims more aggressively when failures occur.
