# The Problem — v11

**Eyebrow:** The Problem

**Heading:** Lost baggage is a visibility failure.

**Subheading:**
The tools the industry operates today cover the checkpoints. They don't see what happens between them. That's a structural gap — not an operational failure.

---

**The ramp is outside the system**
Compliance scanning records when a bag was accepted and when it was delivered. The ramp — where bags move from the BHS carousel to the aircraft hold — sits between those two records. Manual paper reconciliation fills that gap. When it fails, the system has no signal and no alert.

**The transfer gap is structural**
IATA Resolution 753 mandates tracking at four points per bag. Transfer and ramp loading — where 58% of mishandling concentrates — fall between those points. When a bag changes carriers, terminals, or flights, it passes through a zone where no mandatory scan applies and no monitoring infrastructure exists.

**Compliance and operational intelligence are not the same thing**
A bag that clears every mandatory checkpoint but doesn't arrive at destination will generate a WorldTracer case, a claim, and a compensation event. Those systems record failures after the fact. Preventing a failure requires real-time visibility in the gaps — something compliance scanning was never designed to provide.

**Data gaps become liability gaps**
Interline mishandling responsibility defaults to the carrier that cannot prove custody. Cargo theft has no recoverable data point without continuous tracking. Every unmonitored gap in the baggage chain is a gap in accountability — for costs, for claims, and for the operational decisions that depend on knowing where bags actually are.
