# Passenger Compensation Regulations — Global Reference

Regulatory reference on airline liability for mishandled baggage: delayed delivery, damage, and total loss. Covers the primary international frameworks, regional implementations, and the interactions between them.

This document is intended as a working reference for BagMonitor's sales, strategy, and product teams — not as legal advice. All figures are current as of June 2026 and should be verified before use in formal proposals.

---

## Overview: The Regulatory Landscape

Airline liability for baggage is governed by a layered framework. No single global rule applies everywhere. Instead, multiple instruments interact depending on the route, the airline's registration, and where the incident is adjudicated.

The four main instruments:

| Instrument | Scope | Type |
|---|---|---|
| **Montreal Convention 1999** | International carriage between signatory states | Treaty |
| **US DOT — 14 CFR § 254.4** | US domestic routes | Federal regulation |
| **EC Regulation 2027/97 (amended 889/2002)** | EU-departing flights and EU carriers globally | EU secondary law implementing Montreal |
| **IATA Resolution 753** | All IATA member airline operations | Industry standard — NO compensation provisions |

The Montreal Convention is the spine of the global system. US DOT and EC Regulation 2027/97 are domestic/regional implementations that supplement it. R753 has no role in passenger compensation.

## 1. Montreal Convention 1999 (MC99)

### What It Is

The Convention for the Unification of Certain Rules for International Carriage by Air, signed in Montreal on May 28, 1999, replaced the Warsaw Convention of 1929 and its amendments as the primary international treaty governing airline liability. It entered into force on November 4, 2003.

As of 2026, MC99 has been ratified by over 130 states, covering virtually all major aviation markets including the United States, all EU member states, Canada, Australia, Japan, Brazil, Argentina, Colombia, Chile, Mexico, and most other IATA member countries.

### Scope — When Does MC99 Apply?

MC99 applies to **international carriage**: any carriage where the point of departure and the point of destination are situated in two different states that are both parties to the Convention — or where both points are in the same state party but there is an agreed stopping place in another state.

It does **not** apply to purely domestic routes within a single country. Those are governed by domestic law (which may mirror MC99, or may diverge).

### Article 22(2) — Baggage Liability

The core provision for baggage is Article 22(2):

> "In the carriage of baggage, the liability of the carrier in the case of destruction, loss, damage or delay is limited to 1,000 Special Drawing Rights for each passenger unless the passenger has made, at the time when the checked baggage was handed over to the carrier, a special declaration of interest in delivery at destination and has paid a supplementary sum if the case so requires."

*Note: The 1,000 SDR figure in the treaty text is the original limit set when MC99 entered into force in 2003. It has since been revised upward multiple times under the five-year review mechanism of Article 24.*

### Current SDR Limit (December 28, 2024)

**1,519 SDRs per passenger** — effective December 28, 2024.

This is the **fourth revision** since the Convention entered into force. The progression:

| Effective Date | SDR Limit | Change |
|---|---|---|
| November 4, 2003 (entry into force) | 1,000 SDRs | Baseline |
| December 30, 2009 | 1,131 SDRs | +13.1% |
| December 28, 2019 | 1,288 SDRs | +13.9% |
| **December 28, 2024** | **1,519 SDRs** | **+18.0%** |

ICAO announced the 2024 revision on October 18, 2024, using the reference exchange rate of 1 SDR = US$1.33318 on that date, yielding approximately **US$2,026**. At February 2026 exchange rates (~$1.377 USD/SDR), the equivalent reaches approximately **US$2,091**.

Working approximation for sales and business case purposes: **~$2,000–2,100 USD per passenger**.

The review mechanism is codified in Article 24 of MC99: every five years, ICAO calculates the cumulative CPI change for the member states with the highest volume of international air transport and adjusts the SDR limits accordingly. The adjustment takes effect six months after ICAO notifies member states.

Source: ICAO official announcement, October 18, 2024; Condon & Forsyth LLP (aviation law); Clyde & Co. aviation practice.

### What MC99 Covers

Article 22(2) creates a **unified limit** across four event types. There is no separate lower cap for delayed bags or a separate higher cap for lost bags — the 1,519 SDR ceiling applies regardless of whether the baggage was:

- Destroyed
- Lost (total)
- Damaged
- Delayed in delivery

The carrier's liability is strict for destruction, loss, or damage (Article 17): the carrier is liable unless it proves the damage resulted solely from the nature or inherent defect of the baggage itself. For delay (Article 19), the carrier is liable unless it proves it took all reasonable measures, or that it was impossible to take such measures.

### Special Declaration of Interest (Raising the Limit)

Passengers may raise the effective liability ceiling above 1,519 SDRs by making a **special declaration of interest** at check-in. Under Article 22(2):

- The passenger declares the value of the checked baggage at the time of handover
- The carrier may require a supplementary fee
- The carrier's liability is then capped at the declared value (not 1,519 SDRs), unless the carrier proves the declared value exceeds the passenger's actual interest

In practice, airlines commonly offer this as "excess valuation" at check-in counters. It is the passenger's mechanism for insuring high-value baggage beyond the standard limit.

### Procedural Requirements — Notice and Limitation Periods

Failure to meet these deadlines extinguishes the passenger's claim:

| Notice Type | Deadline | Article |
|---|---|---|
| Damage to checked baggage | **7 days** from receipt | Article 31(2) |
| Delay in delivery | **21 days** from date the baggage was placed at the passenger's disposal | Article 31(3) |
| Legal action | **2 years** from the date of arrival, date the aircraft ought to have arrived, or the date carriage stopped | Article 35(1) |

The 2-year limitation period for legal action is absolute under Article 35: the method of calculating the period is determined by the law of the court seized of the case, but the period itself cannot be extended by agreement of the parties (Article 35(2)).

A passenger who fails to file written notice within 7 days (damage) or 21 days (delay) loses the right to claim — with one exception: fraud by the carrier. In practice, airlines typically communicate proactively to avoid disputes over notice timing.

### Jurisdictional Notes — Where MC99 Applies vs. Domestic Law

| Country/Region | Domestic instrument | Relationship to MC99 |
|---|---|---|
| EU member states | EC Regulation 2027/97 (amended by 889/2002) | Directly incorporates MC99 limits; applies to all EU-departing flights and all EU carriers globally |
| United States (international) | Montreal Convention directly | US ratified MC99 in 2003; applies to all international carriage to/from the US |
| United States (domestic) | 14 CFR § 254.4 | Separate federal regulation; $4,700 floor; not governed by MC99 |
| Canada | Carriage by Air Act (incorporating MC99) | Applies to international carriage |
| Argentina | Law 17,285 (Código Aeronáutico) + MC99 ratification | Both apply; MC99 governs international routes |
| Brazil | Civil Aviation Code (Law 7,565) + MC99 ratification | Both apply; MC99 governs international routes |
| Colombia | Aeronautical Code + MC99 ratification | Both apply; domestic routes governed by Colombian regulation |
| Chile | Aeronautical Code + MC99 ratification | Both apply |

## 2. United States — 14 CFR § 254.4

### What It Is

Title 14, Code of Federal Regulations, Part 254 ("Domestic Baggage Liability") establishes the minimum liability floor for US domestic airline operations. It is administered by the US Department of Transportation (DOT).

### Current Limit

**$4,700 per passenger** — effective January 22, 2025.

Published in Federal Register document 2024-23588 (89 FR 84819), dated October 24, 2024. Calculated via CPI-U indexing from the original 1999 baseline of $2,500.

### Critical Distinction: Floor, Not Ceiling

The exact statutory language of § 254.4:

> "An air carrier shall not limit its liability for provable direct or consequential damages resulting from the disappearance of, damage to, or delay in delivery of a passenger's personal property... to an amount less than $4,700 for each passenger."

DOT enforcement guidance has consistently interpreted this to mean the $4,700 is a **minimum floor** — airlines may not limit liability below it, but passengers may claim provable damages above it. There is no statutory ceiling on US domestic baggage liability. A passenger whose provable losses exceed $4,700 may claim the full amount.

This makes US domestic liability structurally different from the Montreal Convention, which establishes a hard ceiling (subject to special declaration). Under MC99, an airline can pay nothing above 1,519 SDRs regardless of proven loss. Under 14 CFR § 254.4, the airline has no equivalent protection.

### Scope — What Routes 14 CFR § 254.4 Covers

- Domestic scheduled and charter air service on large aircraft (more than 60 passenger seats)
- Small-aircraft segments that are ticketed on the same itinerary as a large-aircraft segment

**It does not apply** to international routes to/from the US. Those are governed by MC99.

### The Asymmetry Problem for Transborder Itineraries

This creates a jurisdiction-sensitive discontinuity for airlines operating mixed domestic + international itineraries on a single ticket:

- **International leg (e.g., New York → London):** MC99 governs. Liability ceiling ~$2,000–2,100 USD.
- **Domestic leg (e.g., Dallas → New York, on same ticket):** 14 CFR § 254.4 governs. Minimum floor $4,700. No ceiling on proven damages.

The US domestic floor is now nearly 2.3× the international ceiling under MC99. An airline that loses a bag on the Dallas → New York segment of an international itinerary faces substantially higher liability exposure than if the loss occurred on the transatlantic leg. The legal rule governing which segment applies is fact-specific and not definitively settled by available sources.

### Mandatory Notice to Passengers

Airlines subject to 14 CFR § 254.4 must include a notice of the applicable liability limit in their ticket contracts or baggage check documentation. The required notice language is specified in § 254.5.

### Historical Progression of the US Domestic Limit

| Effective Date | Limit | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| 1999 | $2,500 | Original baseline |
| 2015 | $3,400 | CPI-U adjustment |
| 2021 | $3,800 | CPI-U adjustment |
| January 22, 2025 | **$4,700** | CPI-U adjustment |

Source: 14 CFR § 254.4 (current text); Federal Register 2024-23588.

## 3. European Union — EC Regulation 2027/97 and EC Regulation 889/2002

### What It Is

The EU implements the Montreal Convention through:
- **EC Regulation 2027/97** (October 9, 1997) — the original EU implementation of airline liability rules
- **EC Regulation 889/2002** — amendment that updated Regulation 2027/97 to align with MC99 after the Convention entered into force

Together, these regulations directly incorporate the Montreal Convention limits (now 1,519 SDRs) as EU law.

### Scope

EU baggage liability rules apply to:
- All air carriers licensed in the EU, for all carriage they perform (domestic and international)
- All air carriers operating from an airport located in an EU member state, regardless of where the carrier is registered

This means a US airline flying from Frankfurt is subject to the EU framework for that departure, even if it is not EU-licensed.

### What the EU Framework Adds Beyond MC99

The EU regulations add consumer-facing disclosure obligations:
- Airlines must include a summary of liability rules in their ticket documentation
- The notice must specify the applicable SDR limits
- Airlines must not claim exemptions not permitted under MC99

The EU framework does not raise the liability caps above the MC99 limits. The 1,519 SDR ceiling applies within the EU precisely as it does under MC99 directly.

### The EC 261/2004 Confusion — Critical Clarification

**EC 261/2004 does not apply to baggage mishandling.**

This is one of the most common misunderstandings in the regulatory landscape. EC 261/2004 governs:
- Denied boarding due to overbooking
- Flight cancellations
- Long flight delays (at the departure gate)

It establishes fixed cash compensation (€250–€600 depending on distance) and care obligations (meals, accommodation) for those flight-related disruptions. Baggage — whether delayed, lost, or damaged — is explicitly outside its scope.

Baggage compensation in the EU flows exclusively through Montreal Convention Article 22(2) as implemented by EC Regulation 2027/97. The 1,519 SDR ceiling applies, not any EC 261/2004 fixed amount.

**When a passenger arrives at their destination but their bag doesn't, EC 261 gives them nothing for the bag. MC99 is their only remedy.**

## 4. IATA Resolution 753 — What It Is Not

Resolution 753 is included here specifically because it is frequently and incorrectly cited in discussions of passenger compensation.

### What R753 Actually Is

IATA Resolution 753 (effective June 2018) is an **operational tracking standard** mandating that airlines record baggage custody events at a minimum of four tracking points:

1. Check-in / acceptance by the airline
2. Loading onto the aircraft
3. Transfer between flights (interline)
4. Arrival / delivery to reclaim

The technical mechanism for recording these events is left to each airline — barcode scan, RFID, or other means.

### R753's Financial Mechanism

The only financial consequence R753 creates is an **inter-airline proration penalty**: if an airline cannot demonstrate custody through tracking data, it bears 100% of mishandling costs when operating as part of an interline itinerary, regardless of which carrier actually caused the failure.

This is a B2B cost allocation mechanism between airlines. It has no passenger-facing component.

### R753 and Passenger Compensation — Explicit Disconnect

R753 creates **zero passenger compensation obligations**. A passenger whose bag is mishandled cannot invoke R753 to claim compensation. Their rights flow exclusively from the Montreal Convention and applicable domestic law.

The IATA R753 Implementation Executive Report 2025 describes the standard's stated financial benefits as "significant cost savings through reduced mishandling and more accurate interline baggage proration" — not as consumer protection or compensation.

**R753 should never be cited as a source of passenger rights in BagMonitor sales materials.**

## 5. Latin America — Regulatory Status

### General Rule

All major Latin American aviation markets with significant international operations (Brazil, Argentina, Chile, Colombia, Mexico, Peru, Uruguay) are parties to the Montreal Convention. The 1,519 SDR limit applies to their international routes in the same way it applies everywhere else MC99 is in force.

### Argentina

Argentina ratified the Montreal Convention. For international routes, the 1,519 SDR limit applies. For domestic routes, Argentina's Código Aeronáutico (Law 17,285) governs, with its own liability provisions that may not mirror MC99. The national civil aviation authority is ANAC (Administración Nacional de Aviación Civil).

### Brazil

Brazil ratified the Montreal Convention. For international routes, the 1,519 SDR limit applies. Domestic routes are governed by the Brazilian Aeronautical Code (Law 7,565) and supplemented by ANAC Resolução 400/2016, which established passenger rights including baggage delay compensation. Domestic Brazilian baggage compensation rules are separate from MC99 and have their own provisions — the specific monetary limits under Resolução 400/2016 were not verified through primary sources in this research.

### Colombia

Colombia is a Montreal Convention party. For international routes, 1,519 SDR applies. For domestic compensation, Colombia's aeronautical regulations apply. The specific domestic monetary cap or SDR formula could not be verified through primary regulatory sources in this research — claims referencing Colombian-specific figures did not meet evidentiary standards during adversarial verification.

### Chile

Chile ratified the Montreal Convention. International routes follow the 1,519 SDR limit. Sky Airlines (a Chilean low-cost carrier) confirmed using the $150 IATA operational benchmark for their internal calculations.

### What Remains Unverified for LATAM

No verified, country-specific domestic baggage compensation monetary caps were confirmed through primary regulatory sources for any Latin American jurisdiction. For sales conversations in the region, the conservative approach is to use the MC99 1,519 SDR limit for international routes and flag domestic regulations as requiring per-country legal verification.

## 6. How the Regimes Interact — Hierarchy and Priority

### International Routes (Between Two MC99 Countries)

Montreal Convention governs. The 1,519 SDR ceiling applies. Domestic law may supplement MC99 but cannot reduce the passenger's rights below MC99's floor of protections.

### US Domestic Routes

14 CFR § 254.4 governs exclusively. The $4,700 floor applies. No ceiling on proven losses.

### EU-Departing Flights

EC Regulation 2027/97 (incorporating MC99) applies. The 1,519 SDR ceiling applies. EC 261/2004 does not apply to baggage.

### Conflict Rules

MC99 Article 49 states that any provision in a contract of carriage that derogates from MC99's rules is void. Airlines cannot contractually reduce passenger rights below the MC99 floor for international carriage.

Where a domestic regulation (like US DOT's $4,700) provides greater protection than MC99, the domestic regulation applies to domestic carriage. MC99 applies to international carriage. There is no conflict — they govern different route categories.

### The Special Case of Mixed Itineraries

A single ticket combining a domestic US leg with an international leg creates the most complex interaction:

- The international leg: MC99 governs. Ceiling ~$2,000–2,100 USD.
- The domestic US leg: 14 CFR § 254.4 governs. Floor $4,700; no ceiling.

Which law governs a bag that disappears somewhere on a mixed itinerary — without certainty about which leg the loss occurred on — is a fact-specific legal question. It is not definitively answered by available regulatory texts and would depend on the jurisdiction of the court and the specific facts of the itinerary.

## 7. The Claim Rate Reality

The regulatory maximums cited above represent what airlines could owe under the most favorable interpretation for passengers. What airlines actually pay is substantially different because:

1. **Many passengers don't file claims** — particularly for delayed bags that eventually arrive (even days late). Iberia confirmed: "Hay pasajeros que ni siquiera saben que pueden reclamar por un retraso."
2. **Most events are delays, not permanent losses** — and compensation for delay tends to be lower than for loss.
3. **Airlines negotiate and settle** — formal claims rarely reach the regulatory maximum; most are settled at amounts that satisfy the passenger without approaching the ceiling.
4. **Claims handling is a cost center** — the staff time and process overhead of managing a claim is a real cost on top of the eventual payout.

No verified average payout-per-event data by category (delayed vs. lost vs. damaged) survived adversarial verification in this research. The $90/$180/$950 breakdown sometimes cited in aviation industry blogs did not meet evidentiary standards. Treat any per-category payout figure from secondary sources with caution.

## 8. Field Evidence from Airline Conversations

### Sky Airlines (Juan, Hub Operations — Santiago / Lima)

> "Tomamos como referencia el estándar de IATA, que estima unos 150 dólares por cada caso de mishandling (demoras y daños), incluyendo el traslado y el personal operativo."

Key interpretation: Juan's $150 explicitly covers operational costs only (traslado + personal). He did not include passenger compensation in this number — it is a separate economic layer that Arnold, his colleague, raised independently.

### Sky Airlines (Arnold, International Airports — Argentina / Brazil)

> "El volumen de equipaje extraviado impacta económicamente por las compensaciones y daña muchísimo nuestro NPS (Net Promoter Score)."

Arnold treats compensation as a distinct economic concern from the operational cost — confirming the three-layer stack (operational + WorldTracer + compensation) in practice.

### Iberia (Carolina, Innovation Department — Madrid)

> "El manejo de equipaje es un gran problema, se nos va muchísimo dinero en indemnizaciones y nos pega directo en el NPS del pasajero. Lo que más impacta es la pérdida total, pero lo que sucede en la gran mayoría de los casos es el retraso (y a veces un retraso te lo terminan reclamando como pérdida). Hay pasajeros que ni siquiera saben que pueden reclamar por un retraso."

Key insights from Iberia:
- Indemnization costs are a primary pain point, described as "muchísimo dinero" — material enough to name first
- Total loss is the highest-cost individual event, but delay is the most frequent category by volume
- A meaningful proportion of delay events are escalated into loss claims, creating a cost multiplication effect
- Claim rate is imperfect — some compensation liability never materializes because passengers are unaware of their rights (this is a passenger protection failure, not a cost benefit for airlines)

## 9. Practical Implications for BagMonitor

### The Compensation Layer Is Real but Unquantifiable Per-Airline Without NDA

Every airline we have spoken to confirms that passenger compensation is a real, material cost — Iberia named it first in the conversation, Sky Airlines confirmed it as a separate economic impact from operational costs. But the actual figures are held as confidential commercial data requiring NDA.

This means BagMonitor cannot realistically present a specific compensation savings number in a first meeting. The correct approach:

1. Lead with the $150 confirmed operational baseline — fully documentable, no NDA required. Note WorldTracer ($22) as a separate, conditional cost that applies when a bag enters active tracing
2. Frame compensation as a separate, additional layer using the regulatory figures: "Airlines are exposed to up to $2,000–2,100 per passenger under the Montreal Convention for international routes, and $4,700 on US domestic routes — your actual claims data will tell us what portion of that exposure you're realizing"
3. Invite the prospect to layer in their own claims data for a customized business case

### R753 Compliance Does Not Protect Airlines from Compensation Claims

Airlines that are fully R753 compliant still face the same MC99 compensation liability when bags are mishandled. R753 reduces the inter-airline proration exposure on interline segments — it does not reduce passenger compensation obligations. A bag that fails to load (17% of mishandling events) or gets sent to the wrong transfer point (41%) causes the same regulatory compensation liability regardless of whether the airline's scanning compliance is perfect.

BagMonitor addresses the operational gaps that both R753 compliance scanning and passenger compensation liability share: the 58% of failures that happen between mandatory scan points.
