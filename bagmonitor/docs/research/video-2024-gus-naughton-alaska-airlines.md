# Gus Naughton (Alaska Airlines): Smart Tech and Rapid Prototyping

**Source:** YouTube — https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SLje4qJ9soI
**Date:** c. 2024 (approximately 2 years prior to recording date)
**Speaker:** Gus Naughton — Innovation Engineering Lead, Alaska Airlines (~5 years at the airline)

---

## Role and mandate

Naughton leads Alaska Airlines' Innovation practice from an engineering standpoint. His team looks 2–5 years into the future to evaluate new technological capabilities, aiming to "de-risk" ideas in a sandbox environment before the airline makes substantial financial investments.

## Smart tech & rapid prototyping

His team uses inexpensive, off-the-shelf **consumer-grade hardware** to build rapid prototypes. Example: a makeshift wooden airport gate with generic motion sensors and Amazon Philips Hue smart lights, used to measure passenger boarding rates (guests-per-minute) and test behavioral theories — such as whether playing music at a specific BPM speeds up the boarding process. Cheap proof-of-concept before risking disruption on real flights.

## Key baggage innovations

**Electronic Bag Tags**
- Shifts the bag-check process from the airport to the passenger's home.
- Passengers tap their smartphone to the tag up to 24 hours in advance to update the barcode and routing.
- The physical airport drop-off becomes a sub-30-second transaction.
- A 500-guest trial failed in 2015; a re-test seven years later succeeded because the technology had sufficiently matured.

**Computer Vision at Gates**
- Cameras automatically count carry-on bags boarding aircraft (e.g., the 737).
- Prevents agents from unnecessarily asking passengers to gate-check bags when overhead bin space is still available.
- Benefits: improved turnaround times, more accurate fuel calculations (knowing actual hold commodities), and dramatically better passenger experience.

## Innovation culture

Naughton defines successful innovation as the intersection of three factors: **new business models + new technological capabilities + improved customer or employee experiences**. He insists on involving executive sponsors and subject-matter experts from "day zero" — nothing developed in operational isolation. Failure during testing is expected and necessary; identifying limits is a step toward finding truly scalable solutions.
